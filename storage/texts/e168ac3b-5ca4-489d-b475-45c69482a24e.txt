○ Indicates drainage lines, tanks, and ponding zones

Static topography & hydrology (most reliable —
inherently non-seasonal)
1. Elevation (DEM value) — water collects in low elevation.
How: SRTM/AWS DEM → QGIS raster.
2. Slope — flat areas favor permanent water.
How: QGIS Raster → Terrain analysis → Slope.
3. Curvature / Plan curvature — helps separate depressions vs. ridges.
How: QGIS terrain tools.
4. Flow accumulation / Flow direction / Drainage network — identifies channels and
ponding zones.
How: QGIS SAGA or GRASS hydrology tools from DEM.
5. Topographic Wetness Index (TWI) — static proxy for potential soil moisture /
saturation.
How: derive from flow accumulation and slope in QGIS.
6. Catchment area / Upslope contributing area — larger contributing area implies
persistent hydrology.
How: use watershed tools on DEM.
2) Long-term / composite spectral features (make
non-seasonal by using multi-year statistics)
7. Median NIR, SWIR, Red (multi-year median) — stable spectral signature; water → low
NIR/SWIR.
How: build multi-year Sentinel-2 median composite (cloud-masked) in SNAP or Google
Earth Engine; export to QGIS.
8. Median MNDWI / MNDWI median — water index but use median across years to
remove seasonal ponds.
How: compute MNDWI per scene then take median.
9. Inter-quartile range (IQR) or SD of NDWI across years — low variance implies
perennial water (useful as a feature: low SD → stable water or stable land).
How: create per-pixel stddev or IQR raster from stack.
10. Maximum water-likelihood (e.g., % of time pixel classified as water across years)
— a permanence probability.
How: classify each date then compute percent occurrence (makes “permanent water”
feature).
3) Radar (SAR) features — robust to clouds, less affected
by seasonality if using multi-year)
11. Median VV / VH backscatter (Sentinel-1) — water usually shows very low backscatter
(specular).
How: process Sentinel-1 in SNAP (orbit file, thermal noise, speckle filter), stack and take
median.
12. VV/VH ratio or difference — can separate vegetation / wet soil from open water.
How: compute raster math on radar bands.
13. GLCM texture on SAR median (contrast, homogeneity) — water gives distinct
low-texture signature.
How: compute GLCM in QGIS/SAGA on SAR median.
4) Morphological & shape features (derived from
candidate water polygons)
14. Polygon compactness / elongation / area — reservoirs, tanks, and natural lakes have
different shapes than ephemeral puddles.
How: convert candidate raster to polygons in QGIS and compute shape metrics in
attribute table.
15. Edge sharpness (boundary contrast) — permanent water often has sharp spectral
contrast with surrounding land.
How: use focal filters or compute boundary NDWI gradient.
5) Proximity / contextual features (static)
16. Distance to mapped perennial streams — ponds adjacent to perennial streams more
likely permanent.
How: derive stream network from DEM or use existing hydro layers; compute distance
raster.
17. Distance to roads / settlements — man-made tanks often near infrastructure (useful
for distinguishing tanks vs. natural depressions).
How: use vector layers + QGIS Distance to nearest hub.
18. Soil type / Infiltration class — clayey soils favor longer water retention.
How: attach soil map polygons as categorical features.
19. Geology / lithology — bedrock vs alluvium affects permanence.
How: use state geological maps as vector attributes.
6) Landcover / ancillary stable layers
20. Land use / landcover (long-term product) — e.g., irrigated lands vs. natural
vegetation; stable LULC layers help contextualize.
How: use an official LULC mosaic or multi-year mode LULC.
21. Permanent water mask (global products) — e.g., JRC Global Surface Water
permanence raster — use as a feature (treat as prior).
How: ingest as a categorical raster layer (if allowed).
